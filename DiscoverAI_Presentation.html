Reducing the 5-Star Bias
Balanced sampling: from 61.7% to 46.2% five-star reviews
Why this matters
Without balancing, the model sees a corpus where 6 out of 10 reviews say "5 stars, love it" — useful signal drowns in praise. The bias correction is per-product, so even niche items keep a critical voice.
How the cap works
{5:5, 4:3, 3:2, 2:3, 1:3}
Per product, take at most this many reviews per rating, prioritising the most helpful within each band.

Architecture · Choice #1
Two-tower embedding: product and reviews kept separate
Two sources, two voices
Product text says what the brand sells; reviews say how it actually works. Concatenating lets the noisier source dominate.
Same encoder, two embeddings
all-mpnet-base-v2 (768 dim, trained on 1B+ pairs): shared semantics, vectors we can weight separately.
Weighted review aggregation
70% helpful_vote score + 30% deviation from neutral rating: most useful reviews surface first.
PRODUCT
Title + Brand · Features + Specs
REVIEWS
weighted avg (helpful + rating dist.)
↓     ↓
COMBINED
768-dim, normalised
combined = α · product_emb + (1 − α) · review_emb
α is per-product and dynamic (see next slide)

Choices We Defend
The architectural decisions we would make again
Two-tower over concatenation
Mixing product and review text in one 512-token sequence lets the noisier source dominate. Two towers let us weight contributions explicitly.
Dynamic α over fixed α
An average weight punishes products with credible review evidence and inflates those with thin reviews. Per-product α respects both ends.
Exact FAISS over approximate
At 7,593 products, exact is effectively instant and recall = 1. We adopt approximate when the catalogue forces it, not before.
Iteration v1→v2→v3→v4 over big bang
Each version answers an observed failure (negation, synonyms, hybrid fusion). Clear responsibilities and easy regression testing.
Balanced sampling + 10-review threshold
Cuts the 5-star bias from 62% to 46% and excludes products whose review signal is too thin to generate a reliable embedding.
Extractive fallback for BART
On few reviews, abstractive summarization hallucinates. Extractive is less elegant but honest about the evidence.
